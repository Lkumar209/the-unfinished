The Last Light on Meridian Street

The house at the end of Meridian Street had been dark for eleven years. Not empty — Mrs. Bellacourt was still in there, everyone knew that. You could see the flicker of her television through the gap in the curtains if you walked by at exactly the right angle, late enough that the streetlights had come on but early enough that she hadn't yet retreated to the second floor. The neighborhood kids called it the ghost house, which was unimaginative but not entirely wrong.

Lena Chen had lived three doors down for her entire life and had never once spoken to Mrs. Bellacourt. This was not unusual. Nobody on Meridian Street spoke to Mrs. Bellacourt. The mail carrier left packages on the porch and they disappeared sometime between midnight and dawn. The lawn service came on Tuesdays — always Tuesdays — and the crew worked in silence, as if they'd been instructed not to make conversation with anyone who might happen to be watching from inside.

What was unusual was that Lena was now standing on Mrs. Bellacourt's porch at 6:47 PM on a Thursday in October, holding a casserole dish that was slowly burning through her oven mitts, trying to decide whether to knock or to turn around and pretend this had never occurred to her.

The casserole had been her mother's idea. "Someone should check on her," her mother had said, in the way that mothers say things when they mean someone else should do it but are willing to accept credit for the suggestion. Lena's mother had been saying this for eleven years. The difference was that Lena was now twenty-three and had recently developed the inconvenient habit of actually doing things people suggested, a side effect of a self-help book she'd read about "radical action" that she was beginning to regret.

She knocked.

Nothing happened for long enough that Lena began composing the speech she would give her mother about how she'd tried, she'd really tried, but Mrs. Bellacourt simply wasn't interested in casseroles or human contact and that was her right as an American and a homeowner and they should respect her boundaries.

Then the door opened.

Mrs. Bellacourt was smaller than Lena had imagined. She'd built the woman up in her mind as something substantial — a presence that could fill a house and keep an entire street at arm's length for over a decade. But she was barely five feet tall, with white hair pulled back so tightly it seemed to stretch the skin of her forehead, and eyes that were not unkind but were deeply, almost aggressively, uninterested.

"Yes?" she said.

"I brought you a casserole," Lena said, and immediately wished she had prepared something better. A speech. A reason. Anything other than the blunt fact of a warm dish.

Mrs. Bellacourt looked at the casserole. She looked at Lena. She looked back at the casserole.

"It's chicken and rice," Lena added, as if the specific variety of casserole might be the deciding factor.

"I can see that," Mrs. Bellacourt said. And then, with the air of someone making a decision she would almost certainly regret: "You'd better come in."

The interior of the house was not what Lena expected. She had imagined something Miss Havisham — cobwebs, dust, the accumulated debris of a decade of isolation. Instead it was immaculate. Sparse, even. The furniture was minimal and carefully chosen. The walls were bare except for a single large photograph in the hallway that Lena couldn't quite make out in the dim light. The whole place smelled of lemon cleaner and something else, something older, like the pages of a book left open too long.

Mrs. Bellacourt led her to the kitchen, which was the warmest room in the house — not in temperature but in feeling. There were signs of life here that the rest of the house concealed. A mug with a tea bag still in it. A crossword puzzle folded open on the counter, half completed in blue ink. A small radio on the windowsill, turned off but positioned as if it had been on recently.

"Sit," Mrs. Bellacourt said, gesturing to a chair at the kitchen table. It was not an invitation so much as an instruction.

Lena sat. She placed the casserole on the table between them like a peace offering, which, she supposed, it was.

Mrs. Bellacourt sat across from her and studied her face with an expression Lena couldn't read. It wasn't hostile. It wasn't warm. It was something closer to assessment — the look of a person trying to determine whether another person was worth the energy of a conversation.

"You're the Chen girl," she said. Not a question.

"Lena. Yes."

"Your mother sent you."

"She suggested it. I decided to come."

"There's a difference?"

Lena thought about this. "I think so. She's been suggesting it for eleven years. I only just now decided."

Something shifted in Mrs. Bellacourt's expression. Not quite a smile. A softening, maybe. A recognition of something.

"Eleven years," she repeated. "Is that how long it's been?"

"Since you..." Lena paused, unsure how to finish the sentence. Since you disappeared? Since you stopped existing to the rest of us? Since whatever happened that made you close the door?

"Since I stopped," Mrs. Bellacourt finished for her. "Yes. I suppose it has been about that long."

She stood and walked to the counter, retrieved a second mug, and filled it with water from the kettle. She placed a tea bag in it — Earl Grey, Lena noticed — and set it in front of Lena without asking whether she wanted it.

"You want to know why," Mrs. Bellacourt said, sitting back down.

"I don't want to pry."

"Of course you do. Everyone does. That's why no one comes. They want to know but they don't want to seem like they want to know. You're the first person honest enough or foolish enough to just show up."

Lena wrapped her hands around the mug. The tea was too hot to drink but the warmth felt necessary.

"Okay," she said. "Why?"

Mrs. Bellacourt looked at her for a long moment. Then she looked at the photograph in the hallway — the one Lena had noticed on the way in, the one she still couldn't see clearly from this angle.

"That's a longer answer than you think," she said.

And then she told her.
