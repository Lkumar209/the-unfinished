Why Nobody Finishes Anything Anymore (And Why It's Not Your Fault)

I have seventeen unfinished projects on my laptop right now. I counted them last Tuesday during a procrastination session so recursive it practically folded in on itself — I was procrastinating on a project by counting all the other projects I'd procrastinated on. There's a novel with 34,000 words that stops mid-sentence. A podcast with three recorded episodes and no RSS feed. A woodworking plan for a bookshelf I bought the lumber for eight months ago. A half-built personal website. Two grant applications. A letter to my dead father that I've restarted eleven times.

I used to think this was a character flaw. That I was lazy, or scared, or lacked discipline. The self-help industrial complex certainly wants me to believe this. Set goals. Build habits. Eat the frog. Just ship it. The implication is always the same: finishing is a matter of willpower, and if you can't finish, you are weak.

I don't buy this anymore.

Here's what I think is actually happening: we are living through the greatest explosion of creative access in human history, and absolutely nobody has reckoned with what that means for completion.

Think about it. Fifty years ago, if you wanted to record music, you needed a studio. If you wanted to publish writing, you needed a publisher. If you wanted to make a film, you needed equipment that cost more than a house. The barriers to STARTING were so high that anyone who cleared them had enormous pressure — financial, social, reputational — to FINISH. You didn't walk away from a studio session you'd paid $500 an hour for. You didn't abandon a book deal.

Now starting costs nothing. I can open a Google Doc and begin a novel in thirty seconds. I can download GarageBand and start recording an album before lunch. I can spin up a website, register a domain, start a YouTube channel, buy watercolors, sign up for a pottery class, begin learning Mandarin — all before dinner, all for free or nearly free.

The cost of starting has collapsed to zero. But the cost of finishing hasn't changed at all.

Finishing still requires the same thing it always required: pushing through the part where it stops being fun. The part where the initial vision collides with your actual ability. The part where you realize the thing in your head is better than the thing you're making and you have to decide whether to close that gap through months of unglamorous work or to simply start something new, something that exists only as potential, where the gap between vision and reality hasn't opened yet.

This is the trap. The economic logic of creative work has been completely inverted, and nobody talks about it.

When starting was expensive and finishing was the only way to recoup your investment, the system forced you through the hard part. Now starting is free and there are infinite other things to start, so the moment you hit resistance — the moment the work gets genuinely difficult — the rational move is to abandon it and begin something else. Something fresh. Something where you're still in the honeymoon phase.

I think about this every time someone tells me to "just write." Sure. I can just write. I can just write the beginning of forty things. I'm great at beginnings. Beginnings are where all the possibility lives.

It's middles that kill you.

The middle of a project is where you've committed enough to feel the weight of it but haven't progressed enough to see the end. You know too much about what the thing actually is — as opposed to what you imagined it would be — and that knowledge is almost always deflating. The middle is where you discover that your protagonist is boring, your melody is derivative, your startup idea has a fatal flaw you didn't see from the outside.

And here's the thing nobody tells you:

The middle is where the actual creative work happens.

Beginnings are instinct. Endings are inevitable once you've done the middle. But the middle — the long, unglamorous, doubt-filled middle — is where you develop the skills, make the discoveries, and find the ideas that make the work actually good. The middle is where you earn the thing.

So why do we keep abandoning it?
