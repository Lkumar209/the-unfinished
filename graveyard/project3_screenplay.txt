COLD OPEN

TITLE CARD: "DECEMBER 14"

FADE IN:

INT. COMMUNITY RADIO STATION - BROADCAST BOOTH - NIGHT

A small, cluttered radio booth. Egg-crate foam on the walls, stained with coffee and age. Equipment that was state-of-the-art in 1997. A window looks out onto an empty parking lot dusted with snow.

MAYA TORRES (32) sits behind the mic. She's wearing headphones too large for her head and a flannel shirt with the sleeves rolled up. There's a quality to her — not sadness exactly, but the specific exhaustion of someone who has been awake for reasons she can't explain.

The ON AIR light glows red.

                    MAYA
          It's 2:47 AM on K-W-V-R, community
          radio for the Washoe Valley and
          surrounding areas. You're listening
          to the last hour of The Night Window.
          I'm Maya Torres, and if you're up
          right now, I figure you've either got
          a good reason or a bad one. Either
          way, you're welcome here.

She cues a record — actual vinyl — on a turntable beside her. The needle drops. Nina Simone's "Lilac Wine" fills the booth.

Maya leans back. She looks at the phone on the console. It has not rung tonight. This does not surprise her.

She pulls a small notebook from her bag. Flips to a page filled with handwriting — names, dates, small notations. She studies it, then closes the notebook and puts it away.

The song plays. Maya watches the snow through the window.

                    MAYA (V.O.)
          I started doing the night shift six
          months ago, after David left. Not
          because I couldn't sleep — I could
          sleep fine. Because I wanted to be
          awake when nobody else was. There's
          an honesty to 3 AM that you don't
          get at any other hour. People who
          call in at 3 AM don't want to make
          small talk. They want to say the
          thing they've been not saying all day.

The song ends. Maya leans into the mic.

                    MAYA
          That was Nina Simone. "Lilac Wine."
          If you've never listened to that song
          at three in the morning in an empty
          room, I recommend it. It hits
          different when there's nobody to
          perform your reaction for.

          Our phone lines are open tonight.
          Same number as always. I'm not going
          to beg you to call. You will or you
          won't. But I'm here.

She takes off her headphones. Rubs her eyes. Picks up a cold cup of coffee, considers it, puts it back down.

The phone RINGS.

Maya stares at it. One ring. Two. She puts her headphones back on.

                    MAYA
          K-W-V-R, you're on The Night Window.

A long pause. Breathing.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          Is this... do you take requests?

                    MAYA
          I take whatever you've got.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          I don't actually have a request. I
          just needed to hear someone's voice.
          That's pathetic, right?

                    MAYA
          Not even a little bit.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          I've been sitting in my car in a
          Walmart parking lot for two hours.
          I don't know why I'm telling you
          that. I don't even know why I'm in
          the parking lot. I was driving
          somewhere and I just... stopped.

                    MAYA
          Sometimes you stop because you're
          not sure the place you're driving
          to is where you want to be.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          Yeah. Maybe.

          (pause)

          My wife doesn't know I'm here. She
          thinks I'm at work. I'm supposed
          to be at work. I work nights at
          the distribution center off 395.

                    MAYA
          But you're not at work.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          I haven't been at work in three
          weeks.

The weight of this sits in the air. Maya doesn't rush to fill it.

                    CALLER (V.O.) (CONT'D)
          I get in the car every night at
          10:30 and I drive to the parking
          lot and I sit here until 6 AM and
          then I drive home and I tell her
          it was a normal shift. And I don't
          know why. I didn't get fired. I
          didn't quit. I just stopped going.

                    MAYA
          Do you know what you do in the
          parking lot? For eight hours?

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          I listen to the radio. I watch
          people come and go. The overnight
          stockers. The cleaning crew. People
          who pull in at 4 AM to buy diapers
          or cold medicine. Everyone has a
          reason to be at Walmart at 4 AM
          except me.

                    MAYA
          Maybe your reason is figuring out
          your reason.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          That's generous.

                    MAYA
          I'm a generous person at 3 AM.
          Less so in daylight.

A small laugh from the caller. The first break in the weight.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          What do I do?

                    MAYA
          I don't think that's the right
          question yet.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          What's the right question?

                    MAYA
          What are you not going back to?
          It's not the job. The job is just
          the building you stopped walking
          into. What's the actual thing?

Silence. Long enough that Maya checks the line is still connected.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          I think I have to go.

                    MAYA
          Okay. You know where to find me.
          Same time tomorrow. Same parking lot,
          probably.

                    CALLER (V.O.)
          Yeah. Probably.

CLICK. The line goes dead.

Maya sits with it. She picks up her notebook, writes something. Date, time, a few words. She closes it.

She cues another record.

                    MAYA
          That was a caller who needed a minute.
          We all need a minute sometimes. Here's
          Chet Baker, because it's that kind of
          night.

"Almost Blue" begins to play.

SMASH CUT TO:

INT. MAYA'S APARTMENT - MORNING

Small, clean, impersonal. The apartment of someone who doesn't expect visitors. Morning light through thin curtains.

Maya sits at a kitchen table with coffee and her notebook open. She's reviewing her notes from last night's calls. The caller from the Walmart parking lot has his own page now.

Her phone buzzes. A text from CHRISTINE (sister):

TEXT: "Mom's asking about Christmas again. Are you coming or not. She needs to know for the table."

Maya reads it. Does not respond. She goes back to her notebook.

We see the pages as she flips through them. Dozens of entries. Names (or descriptions — "Walmart caller," "the woman with the dog," "insomnia guy #3"). Dates. Brief notes about what they said, what she heard underneath what they said.

This is not a professional obligation. This is something else.

MAYA (V.O.)
          There's a version of this where I'm
          a therapist, or a social worker, or
          someone qualified to be collecting
          the things people say at 3 AM. I'm
          not any of those things. I have a
          communications degree from a school
          that doesn't exist anymore and a
          part-time job at a radio station
          that barely exists now.

          But here's what I've figured out:
          the people who call in at 3 AM
          aren't looking for a therapist.
          They're looking for a witness.
          Someone to confirm that the thing
          they're feeling is real, that they're
          not making it up, that the weight
          is the weight and not something
          they invented to feel sorry for
          themselves.

          I can do that. I'm good at that.

          What I haven't figured out is what
          to do with everything they've
          told me.

She closes the notebook. Stares at it.

END OF COLD OPEN.

NOTES TO SELF:

- The notebook becomes the central object. She's been collecting these stories for six months. But to what end? Does she want to do something with them? A book? A documentary? Does she feel an obligation to these callers?

- Need to figure out: what is Maya's WANT? She's a great listener, great observer. But what does she DO? What's the engine that drives the rest of the story?

- The Walmart caller needs to come back. He's the through-line. But what happens when he does? Does his situation escalate? Does Maya get involved beyond the mic?

- PROBLEM: I know who Maya is but I don't know what she does next. The setup is strong but there's no second act. She can't just keep taking calls — that's a premise, not a plot. Something needs to happen that forces her out of the booth and into action. What is it??

- Maybe: she finds out one of her callers is in real danger? Too procedural.
- Maybe: the station is getting shut down? Too predictable.  
- Maybe: the Walmart caller stops calling and she goes looking for him? Getting warmer but why would she do that?

- I keep circling. Coming back to this later.
